Traditionally I have written individual reports for each race that I've done. This year (partly due to laziness) I'm going to do a single post that summarizes my entire year of training and races. I'm hoping this might be a better format since it will capture the ups and downs of my year rather than isolated events.

In 2024 I ran my first ever [triathlon](/blog/2024/great-white-north-sprint-triathlon-race-report) and quickly followed it with [another](/blog/2024/do-north-sprint-triathlon-race-report) — by the end of the year I ran my first ever [half marathon](/blog/2024/run-the-river-half-marathon-race-report). I found the race atmosphere electrifying and more importantly I enjoyed discovering what my body and mind was capable of. I was hooked.

In November of 2024 I had found a triathlon coach named Jason that I wanted to work with. The main reason I found a coach was because I was planning to do both an olympic triathlon and a marathon in 2025 and I had no clue how to balance training for both. Having a coach has proved very helpful in other ways: lower mental overhead from planning each week; someone to pose very technical training or race questions to; a small boost of external motivation each week; learning about training camps and races; and someone experienced to help properly manage my weekly load.

Motivated from 2024's races and with a coach by my side I started 2025's training in earnest. I had two main goals for the year that I was working towards — the Vancouver Marathon and beating my brother-in-law Patrick in the Great White North Olympic Triathlon. The marathon was in May and the triathlon was in July. As such I started with a mix of strength, swimming, biking, and running training — but with a heavy focus on running.

Another benefit of having a coach was the variety and specificity of my workouts. For example a swim session for me used to look like picking an arbitrary distance, say 800 m, and trying to just swim that straight up. But, with a coach my sessions had a focus e.g. technique or intervals. They would have structured warmups, main sets, cool downs, specific timing instructions, and more.

Training with a coach also resulted in a marked increase in my weekly training volume. Previously I was training maybe five times a week. Following my coaches plan I was training maybe eight times a week. This feels normal to me now, but it took my body awhile to adjust to that big jump in volume.

Speaking of volume because I had a marathon coming up in four months my running volume was climbing rapidly to amounts I had never run before. At first this felt great, but it quickly took a turn for the worse. I would come back from runs with my glutes feeling absolutely spent. I didn't think much of it at the time, but with hindsight this was a clear sign that injury was right around the corner.

On the first week of February during a company offsite in Cancun injury finally struck. After a run one morning the outside of my right knee felt like it had been stabbed by a knife. I was out of commission for running. Later at physio I learned that I had IT band syndrome. I immediately started rehab which consisted of strengthening my glutes and rolling out my IT band. It turned out that one specific exercise that focused on my gluteus medius was the main fix.

Thankfully even with the injury I was still able to swim and bike mostly pain free so that training continued. Running was an issue though: I desperately needed to increase my running volume if I was going to do the Vancouver Marathon but even with my rehab going well I wasn't able to add volume back at the pace I needed. I ultimately made the tough decision to withdraw from the marathon and transfer my registration to the 8 km race the same day.

I had been planning to run the marathon with my brother-in-law Patrick and a number of his friends. We were all staying together and the energy was exciting. I was gutted that I wasn't doing the marathon with everybody and was feeling down. In a pessimistic mood I decided that I was going to just treat the 8 km as a chill training run. One of Patrick's friends Adam insisted that there was no way that this would happen and that once I heard the anthem I would full send it.

Adam was right. As I lined up in the starting corral the anthem began to play and something came over me. I started at about a 5:10/km which was a minute faster than I was planning to run, but also felt comfortable. After the first two kilometers I just kept picking it up and throwing down negative splits. By the last kilometer I was moving at a good 4:30/km. The pace was challenging for me and my heart was red lining at the end. I remember thinking that the finish line was so far away! But, it was only an 8 km race so I was refreshed quite quickly after and ready to watch my friends complete the marathon. Given the circumstances I was very happy with my result.
